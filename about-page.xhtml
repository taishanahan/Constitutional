Background

The fall of the Soviet Union, a hegemonic cultural and political power in Europe and Asia, produced a veritable explosion of states that rose from its ashes. These fifteen states, Armenia, Azerbaijan, Belarus, Estonia, Georgia, Kazakhstan, Kyrgyzstan, Latvia, Lithuania, Moldova, Russia, Tajikistan, Turkmenistan, Ukraine, and Uzbekistan preceded their socialists republics and are internationally considered the successors of their corresponding Union Republics and not the Soviet Union. The three Baltic states, Estonia, Latvia, Lithuania, prioritized integrating into the international community, which clashed with the near-total disengagement with the Russian-dominated post-Soviet sphere, and eventually joined the European Union (EU) and the North Atlantic Treaty Organization (NATO). The forms of governments of the new states ranges from dominant-party to parlimentan to unitary presidential systems. Economic recessions that followed the USSR’s dissolution marked a period of the fall of the Gosplan, the state planning agency responsible for economic planning, and the rapid embracement of market economic policies. This resulted in “shock therapy” policies in the early 1990s, where trade liberalization, price liberalization, and privatization triggered a drop in GDP and budget cuts towards social programs like education and healthcare. Therefore, some common trends found in the original constitutions drafted for each state was molded by this acceptance of a liberal economy, with instituions like the central bank and private property explicitly mentioned as independent to the state institions. Pairing this with social policies, privatization and liberalization formed the foundations of citizens’ liberties within each constitution. Our analysis, then, is an evaluation of the commitments and prioties within the first sections of the constitutions of these post-Soviet republics and, in turn, how those commitments have been upheld in the following decades.

Methods

For this project, our team chose to analyze just the preamble and first chapter of the constitutions. This was a tough decision for us to make. Our other options including marking up the entirety of all of the documents, or limiting it even more to just the preambles. The drawback of doing the entire document was the time limit. Some of the constitutions came out to be thousands of lines long, and because we were doing out descriptive markup by hand, this would have taken us a long time. However, we seriously considered doing the full texts because by only analyzing the preamble and first chapter, we cannot make full claims about what the constitutions as a whole include or leave out, because it may be in a later chapter. We opted to do only the preamble and the first chapter so that we could analyze which themes were most important to each country.

Our team, to ensure an efficacious work process and acessible reading process, worked to define the elements that comprised the analysis of these documents. These elements are not all-encompassing, but are instead designed to be applicable to the body of text so as to make data collection informative and robust, but also efficient.

  • Individual Rights

    Refers to any explicit mentions the following individual rights. Does not include 'value' commitments.

    Types of Individual Rights

    • Protest - Right to petition or demonstrate against the government.
    • Citizenship - Anything pertaining to citizenship, naturalizations, or the explicit privileges of citizens.
    • Assembly - The right to assembly.
    • Legal - Concerning any legal protections such as reference to self-incrimination or indigent defense systems.
    • Vote - Includes explicit reference to the right to vote.
    • Racial-equality - Anything referring to religious, ethnic, gender, racial, etc equality
    • Liberty - Refers to more abstract mentions of "rights and freedoms" or liberty.
    • Misc - Used to mark other relevant rights, such as healthcare.

  • Nationalism

    Includes mentions of national pride, history, patriotism, or responsibility

  • Religion

    Any mention of religion, it's freedoms, or its role with relation to the state.

    Religion Type

    • Secular - Refers to to either secularism or a separation of church and state.
    • State-religion - Used to mark mentions of a religion or faith that endorsed or even enforced.
    • Neutral - Pertians to more abstract mentions of a god or faith.

  • Government

    Marks references to government structure and design that could not have been generally quanitified otherwise.

  • Sovereignty

    Reference to the right of the individual state's right to exist and its sovereign status.

  • Capital

    Explicit designation of a capital city.

  • Currency

    Explicit designation of a currency.

  • Consent

    Pertains to any mention of the consent of the governed. Generally includes the idea that the government belongs to the people or that state authority lies with the people of the state.

  • Elections

    Reference to electoral proceedings. Not suffrage, but rather what types elections will be held and how.

  • Separation

    Mentions of separation of powers and different branches of government (executive, legislative, judicial).

  • Parties

    Reference to political parties and their definition, permittance, or prohibition.

    Party System

    • 0 - Political Parties are not allowed. There are none.
    • 1 - This is a one-party state.
    • 2 - This is a two-party state.
    • Multi - A plurality of parties may exist in this state.

  • Property

    Reference to rights of public or private ownership.

    Property Type

    • Public - Government ownership of property.
    • Private - Private property, may be individual or corporate.
    • Other - Any kind of mixed ownership arrangement between state and private entities.

  • Language

    Explicit mention of language.

    Language Type

    • Native - Mention of a language specific to the region or culture of this state.
    • Russian - Mention of the Russian language, which was, for a time, the standardized language of the Soviet Union.

  • International

    Any reference to international politics or association. The role the state intends to have internationally.

  • Symbols

    Official symbols representing the state including flags, anthems, coats of arms, etc.

  • Peace

    Reference to harmony or order.

  • Treason

    Reference to attempts to usurp the powers that be in the state. Any attempt to illegitimately remove established government.

  • Levels

    Mention of levels of government (national, state, local, etc), their powers, and how they interact with one another.

    Levels Type

    • Unitary - The federal government is completely dominant over the states and only allows it specifically enumerated rights.
    • Federalist - The federal government and the state government have specific rights that they share. State is left rights which the federal government doesn't define for itself.
    • Neutral - General reference to the levels of government.

  • Economy

    Explicit definition of a state economic system

    Economy Type

    • Free - Not completely free market, but reference to a capitalist, neo-liberal economy similar to the United States.
    • State - Reference to Socialism, Communism, or any control of the economy by the state.
    • Mixed - In between these two. The state will have more control of the economy than in an average capitalist system, but also allows some relatively greater amount of market activity.
